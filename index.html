Never Forgetting Violent Displacement

While this site offers visitors the opportunity to learn about the National Parks in the U.S. nation-state in an in-depth manner, we recognize that national parks are in fact on stolen land. National Parks, while beautiful and important in the conversation around conservation, popular rhetoric often fails to explore erasure and genocide in the name of conversation and the "American" public. Here, we hope to begin that conversation by providing information about the National Parks alongside information about Indigenity and Land. This project is not complete, and we will continue working forward to challenge the dominant narrative that these lands were without people and the myths that they were freely gifted by Indigenous peoples. As you will see on our timeline, one of our goals is to forefront Native names for s/places rather than solely park names. Visit Indigenous Geotags to learn more about role that names have in decolonialization and indigenization. View the National Parks Timeline

Map

Visit our mapping of the U.S. nation-state's National Parks to visualize various parks. Currently, the National Park Service is contending with a decrease in funding. Additionally, the House passed an act in 2017 that makes it difficult to create new national monuments and also allows for presidents to reduce the current size of national monuments (parks included).

Timeline

Our timeline works to place the "establishment" of National Parks alongside historical dates pertaining to land theft and Indigenous histories. While the timeline does not show a complete story, we hope that visualizing the temporal and spatial aspects alongside the sociohistorical will challenge the myth that National Parks are pure, netural spaces.

CHI

The Cultural Heritage Informatics initiative is hosted at Michigan State University through the Department of Anthropology and MATRIX: the Center for Digital Humanities and Social Sciences. As 2018-2019 CHI fellows, we work to construct narratives with data. This project is a part of the Visualization Challenge.
